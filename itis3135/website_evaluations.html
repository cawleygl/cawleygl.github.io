Website Evaluations

Nespresso.com

Nespresso.com is a good example of CRAP principles on the web. The site lists nespresso coffee makers and other items primarily using horizontal sections to separate design elements. The site uses black and white to establish good contrast, and uses different shades of brown to resemble the coffee that they are selling.

  • Contrast: White text on black backgrounds, black text on white backgrounds, and white text on darker brown backgrounds create good contrast so that the text is readable. Background photographs that differ in color create contrast with one another to differentiate between sections of the page.
  • Repetition: Subsections use the same text size and font to note that they are of equal importance. Some sections use a series of buttons that repeat the same style to show that they are options to select between.
  • Alignment: The footer is right-justified, which is unusual but lines up well on the right side. Sections generally use block layout to align large photos across the page, and groups of smaller photos are aligned perfectly on the left and right sides.
  • Proximity: Sections containing selectable groups of options are closely aligned to show that they are members of a group, and larger sections have ample padding to show that sections differ from one another.

Art.Yale.edu

Art.Yale.edu is a poor example of CRAP principles on the web. It is awkwardly organized, brightly colored, and overall difficult to look at. All of the content on the page is pushed to the left side, some content moves in a distracting manner, among plenty of other infractions.

  • Contrast: Some elements have enough contrast to be readable, but far too many elements use colors too bright to be read comfortably. The design of header elements is reused to hold other content, when these should contrast to note the difference in content. Contrast is also used in situations where it shouldn't, as nearby elements that fulfill similar purposes use different colors and designs.
  • Repetition: Some repetition, though sporadic, does work to add a little bit of uniformity, but this use isn't nearly consistent enough to unify the page. Wildly varying designs come and go at whim, many are used only once, and repetition has little to do with the similarities of element content.
  • Alignment: Though elements in the main timeline are generally aligned on the left and right, some are misaligned, and internal alignment of these elements is inconsistent at best. All of the page content is completely left-justified, which wastes a lot of space in larger formats. Smaller elements like buttons are inconsistently aligned.
  • Proximity: The timeline format does practice good proximity, with post content located close together and additional space separating different posts. However, some other related elements are located on opposite ends of the page.
